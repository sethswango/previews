What you can already measure — and the gap

You asked what’s there before spending on more. Two tools are already running.
Live
Framer’s built-in analyticsYour Framer plan (custom domain) includes privacy-first analytics right in the dashboard — visitors, pageviews, top pages, where traffic comes from, devices. No cookie banner needed. It keeps about 30 days of history. Zero setup; open the Analytics tab and it’s there. Best for a quick “how many people, from where, to which pages.”
Live
Google Analytics (GA4) is installed and collectingThe GA4 tag is firing on every page and has been gathering data. It goes deeper than the Framer view: you can mark specific actions as goals (a form fill, a booked call), follow the full path a visitor takes, and it keeps roughly 14 months of history. It just needs someone with access to actually read it.
The gap
Nobody has eyes on either — and search terms aren’t trackedData is piling up that isn’t being turned into decisions. And the one thing that shows what people typed to find you — Google Search Console — isn’t connected yet. That’s the piece that tells us which searches to go after.

The one step that makes everything after it measurable

Here’s the honest limit of this report: everything above is measured from the outside. GA4 is quietly collecting, but without view access we can’t chart real sessions, form fills, or what people search to find you.

That’s also the argument for doing it first. SEO work only means something if you can see it move a number. If we snapshot the baseline — today’s sessions, form fills, and search terms — before the improvements, then every change afterward reads as “we did X, here’s the calls and searches it moved,” instead of asking you to take it on faith. Granting view-only access to Analytics and Search Console takes a couple of minutes and carries no risk to your account. It’s what turns the next version of this page into real before-and-after numbers.

Where we’d want to take this

What each piece does — and, where there are numbers, what they tend to be worth

Set up your Google Business Profile

Claim and verify the profile, seed it with the reviews you already have on paper, add real photos, set your service area and categories. This is the single biggest visibility gap, and the fastest to close — it’s what puts you in the map box for “marketing agency near me” searches.

4×more website visits from a verified profile vs an unclaimed one
~59customer actions/mo on an average local profile (clicks, directions, calls)
3.2%of profile visitors become customers — vs 0.7% from social
And reviews compound it: across local businesses, each additional review is associated with roughly 80 more website visits, 63 direction requests, and 16 phone calls. You have six written reviews ready to seed on day one.

A small, honest numbers report

The two tools you already have, plus Search Console, wired into one plain monthly check: sessions, form fills, booked calls, and what people typed to find you. Not a dashboard you have to log into — a page you glance at.

Minimal by design. A handful of numbers that actually move, not more data than anyone will read. This is also the “before” baseline from the section above, kept current.

Turn your case studies into search content

Each case study can rank for real searches — “logo design springfield mo,” “product photography missouri” — once the pages have proper headings and structured data. The content already exists; this is giving it a shape Google can read.

Starts with the page-heading fix flagged above, then extends to each new study as you build it.

Local links, not cold outreach

The businesses you already shoot and brand are natural link partners — a credit on their site, a mention in their launch post. Combined with fully filling out listings you’re already in, like the Chamber of Commerce.

No paid link schemes, no spam directories — just the relationships that already exist, made visible to search.

None of this needs new tools or a big lift. It’s the same kind of work already sitting in this batch, just kept up on a schedule.
